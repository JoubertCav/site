The model

A compact residual-demand entry game.
Market demand
\(P(Q)=a-bQ\)
A larger total quantity reduces price. The incumbent chooses committed capacity/output \(K\).
Entrant residual demand
\(P_E(q_E;K)=a-b(K+q_E)\)
After the incumbent's commitment, the entrant can sell only into the residual market.
Entrant's best response
\(q_E^*(K)=\max\left\{\dfrac{a-c_E-bK}{2b},0\right\}\)
The entrant enters only if this opportunity is large enough to cover the sunk cost.
Entry-deterrence threshold
\(K^D=\max\left\{\dfrac{a-c_E-2\sqrt{bF_E}}{b},0\right\}\)
Capacity at least \(K^D\) makes entrant variable profit no larger than \(F_E\).

Strategic logic

What makes a barrier credible?
1
Incumbent commits
Capacity is installed before entry and is costly to reverse.
2
Entrant observes
The entrant computes residual demand after the incumbent's committed capacity.
3
Entry test
Enter when variable profit exceeds the sunk entry cost.
4
Price adjusts
Limit pricing is credible when the low price is backed by a sunk commitment.
Sunk costs create hysteresis.

Once entry requires an irreversible cost, the entrant needs strictly positive operating profit before entry is worthwhile.

Excess capacity changes beliefs.

A promise to fight entry is cheap talk unless capacity makes aggressive post-entry output feasible and credible.

Limit pricing is not free.

The incumbent sacrifices current price or pays capacity cost. Deterrence is rational only when this sacrifice is smaller than the loss from entry.
