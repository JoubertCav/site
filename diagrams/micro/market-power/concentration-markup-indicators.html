1. Market shares and HHI

HHI rises when market share is concentrated. The same number of firms can produce very different concentration if shares are asymmetric.

2. The HHI–Lerner bridge

The textbook relation is useful but conditional: with homogeneous goods and Cournot conduct, average Lerner is approximately HHI divided by demand elasticity.

3. Price wedge and welfare geometry

The Lerner index is a marginal-cost wedge. Higher concentration increases this wedge only through the conduct–elasticity channel.

4. Full-cost pricing is different

Accounting markups use average cost. Lerner uses marginal cost. They can move together, but they do not measure the same object.

5. What the indicators say

Use these diagnostics as a map: structure, pricing power, and accounting rules are related but conceptually separate.

Limits of concentration measures

HHI is structure, not conduct.

A concentrated market can have low markups if demand is elastic, entry is easy, imports discipline prices, or firms compete aggressively.

Lerner needs marginal cost.

Estimating marginal cost is hard. Accounting cost, average cost, and allocated overhead are not the same object as marginal cost.

Full-cost pricing can mislead.

A price above average cost may reflect overhead recovery, capacity planning, or accounting conventions rather than monopoly power.

Market definition matters.

HHI changes with the market boundary: geography, product differentiation, imports, platforms, buyer power, and potential entrants all affect interpretation.

A good empirical reading is: HHI gives a first structural signal; Lerner/markup measures pricing power; full-cost rules explain accounting behavior; none is sufficient alone.
