Interpretation

Cartel = monopoly benchmark. Joint-profit maximization chooses the same total output as a monopolist: restrict output, raise price, and create deadweight loss.
Quota allocation. Quotas divide monopoly output. With identical costs, total profit is unchanged by the allocation, but individual enforcement constraints change.
Deviation incentive. A member wants to expand output while rivals obey their quotas. That increases current profit but lowers price for everyone.
Repeated game. Collusion is sustainable only when the discounted future loss from punishment is large enough relative to the one-shot deviation gain.

Exam-style summary

1
Solve joint profit: maximize \((a-bQ-c)Q\) to get \(Q^M\) and \(P^M\).
2
Allocate quotas: \(q_i^C=s_iQ^M\). Equal quotas use \(s_i=1/N\).
3
Deviation: hold rivals fixed at \(Q_{-i}^C\), then maximize \((a-b(Q_{-i}^C+q_i)-c)q_i\).
4
Compare payoffs: cartel payoff \(\pi_i^C\), deviation payoff \(\pi_i^D\), punishment payoff \(\pi_i^P\).
5
Collusion is enforceable when \(\delta\ge\delta^*\). More patient firms, better monitoring, and harsher punishment reduce temptation.
