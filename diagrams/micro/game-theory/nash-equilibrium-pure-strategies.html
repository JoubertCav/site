Interpretation

Best-response intersection. The geometric object in a finite normal-form game is the set of cells in the matrix. A pure Nash equilibrium is a special cell: both players are already responding optimally there.
Coordination games. When incentives align locally, two different cells can both be mutual best responses, generating multiple pure equilibria.
Prisoner's dilemma. Strict dominance drives the equilibrium. The dominant-strategy outcome is a unique Nash equilibrium, even if another cell gives both players higher payoffs.

Exam-style summary

1
For each column, compare the row player's payoffs and mark the larger one.
2
For each row, compare the column player's payoffs and mark the larger one.
3
Any cell with both marks is a pure-strategy Nash equilibrium.
4
If exactly one strategy always beats the other, you have dominance and usually a unique equilibrium.
5
If two cells carry both marks, the game has multiple pure equilibria.
