Mixed Strategies

A mixed equilibrium is not randomness for its own sake. It is an indifference device: each player chooses probabilities that make the opponent just willing to randomize over the support.

Symbolic core

Row's indifference

q a+(1-q)b=q c+(1-q)d

At q*, Row is willing to put positive probability on both U and D.

Column's indifference

p e+(1-p)g=p f+(1-p)h

At p*, Column is willing to put positive probability on both L and R.

Support principle

s\in \operatorname{supp}(\sigma_i)\Rightarrow s\in \arg\max_{t\in S_i} u_i(t,\sigma_{-i})

Best-response square

The Nash equilibrium is a fixed point: the chosen mixture lies inside the opponent's best-response correspondence.

Payoff landscape

The surface is expected Row payoff over mixed profiles. In zero-sum games, equilibrium is the saddle intuition.

What to notice

At an interior mixed equilibrium, nobody is strictly better off changing the pure action used inside her support.

Probe incentive

Move p and q to see which pure strategy becomes the best response away from equilibrium.

Zero-sum check

The matrix is close to zero-sum when each payoff pair approximately sums to zero.

Compact exam summary

2×2 mixed equilibrium
1
Write expected payoffs.Each pure strategy generates an affine payoff line in the opponent’s mixing probability.
2
Use indifference.For Row to mix, Column chooses q so that U and D have equal expected payoff. For Column to mix, Row chooses p so that L and R have equal expected payoff.
3
Check feasibility.If p*, q* ∈ [0,1], the full-support mixed equilibrium is valid. If not, check pure or boundary-support equilibria.
4
Interpret.Equilibrium probabilities are chosen to remove profitable pure deviations from the opponent’s support.
(p^*,q^*)\text{ is Nash}\iff p^*\in BR_R(q^*)\ \text{ and }\ q^*\in BR_C(p^*)
