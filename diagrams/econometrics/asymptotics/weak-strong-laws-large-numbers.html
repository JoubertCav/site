Weak and strong laws of large numbers

Sample averages are projections of many random fluctuations into one number. The weak law is mass concentration of \(\bar X_n\); the strong law is path stabilization of \(\bar X_n(\omega)\).
Sample average
X̄n = n−1∑i=1nXi random path compressed into one running mean
Weak LLN
X̄n → μ in probability mass outside every ε-tube vanishes
Strong LLN
X̄n → μ almost surely almost every path eventually settles
Sample moments
n−1∑g(Xi) → E[g(X)] requires integrability of \(g(X)\)

Interpretation
Weak LLN.
For iid integrable variables, \(P(|\bar X_n-\mu|>\varepsilon)\to0\).
Strong LLN.
For iid integrable variables, \(\bar X_n\to\mu\) almost surely.
Finite variance.
Not needed for the iid strong law, but makes concentration visually faster and Chebyshev-friendly.
Moments.
The sample \(r\)-moment is consistent only when \(E|X|^r<\infty\).
