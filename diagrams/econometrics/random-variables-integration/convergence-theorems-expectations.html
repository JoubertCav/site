Convergence theorems for expectations

Limit-interchange is a geometric question about mass: does mass rise monotonically, stay trapped under an integrable roof, or escape into thinner and taller spikes? These theorems tell when expectation commutes safely with limits.
Monotone convergence
0≤fₙ↑f
∫fₙ dP ↑ ∫f dP
Dominated convergence
fₙ→f, |fₙ|≤g, ∫g dP<∞
∫fₙ dP→∫f dP
Fatou
fₙ≥0
∫liminf fₙ dP ≤ liminf ∫fₙ dP
Unsafe interchange
fₙ→0 pointwise
but ∫fₙ dP↛0
Controls
0≤fₙ↑f ⇒ E[fₙ]↑E[f].

Interpretation
A convergence theorem is a permission rule for moving the limit through the expectation sign.
MCT.
Nonnegative functions grow upward; the areas grow to the limiting area.
DCT.
Functions may oscillate, but an integrable envelope prevents area from escaping.
Fatou.
For nonnegative functions, the limit inferior cannot have more area than the limit inferior of areas.
Danger.
Pointwise convergence alone does not control expectation; thin spikes can keep area.
What to notice
The key question is not only whether fₙ(ω) converges for each ω. The key question is whether the mass is controlled while it converges.
