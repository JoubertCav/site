Distributions as pushforward measures

A random variable transports probability mass from the hidden sample space \(\Omega\) onto the real line. The distribution of \(X\) is the measure induced by the map \(X\), not a separate primitive object.
Random variable
X : (Ω, ℱ, P) → (ℝ, 𝔅(ℝ))
Pushforward law
μX = P ∘ X−1
μX(B)=P(X∈B)
Distribution function
FX(x)=P(X≤x)
=μX((−∞,x])
Expectation transfer
E[g(X)] = ∫ℝ g(x) dμX(x)
Controls
μX(B)=P(X−1(B))
The real-line mass of B equals the Ω-mass of its preimage.
Pushforward geometry
probability starts on Ω X transports mass to ℝ
Right interval B pulls back to X−1(B)
Distribution function
FX(x)=μX((−∞,x])
Why work on the real line?
Events about X become ordinary Borel sets
Interpretation
X sends each hidden state ω to a real number. The law μX records how much original probability lands in each region of the real line.
Law.
μX is the probability measure on ℝ induced by X.
Preimage logic.
To measure B⊂ℝ, pull it back to Ω and use P.
CDF.
FX(x) measures the accumulated pushed mass up to x.
Compression.
Different Ω spaces can induce the same μX; the distribution forgets irrelevant hidden structure.
What to notice
The distribution is not a picture drawn on top of X. It is transported measure: probability mass moves from Ω to ℝ through the map X.
