Hessian and second-order structure

The Hessian is the matrix of second-order behavior. It controls the local quadratic model, the sign of curvature in each direction, and the classification of critical points into minima, maxima, saddles, or degenerate cases.

Quadratic remainder

Quadratic form

The Hessian enters through \(\tfrac12 h^\top Hh\). This term is the first one capable of distinguishing bowl, dome, and saddle behavior once the linear part disappears.

Definiteness

If \(h^\top Hh>0\) for all nonzero \(h\), the quadratic form is positive definite and the graph curves upward in every direction. Negative definite means the opposite. Mixed signs produce saddles.

Eigen-directions

The Hessian's eigenvectors are the principal directions of curvature. Their eigenvalues give the strongest upward and downward second-order bending at the point.

2D contours and principal directions

The contour pattern around \(a\) reveals the second-order type. Near a minimum or maximum the contours are nested ellipses; near a saddle they cross in hyperbolic fashion.

contour geometry

Short explanation

The local contour geometry is the 2D footprint of the quadratic form. The Hessian eigenvectors pick the axes of the local ellipse or hyperbola.

Directional curvature as the angle changes

The function \(u^\top H(a)u\) over the unit circle shows how curvature depends on direction. Its maxima and minima occur along Hessian eigenvectors.

Rayleigh quotient on the circle

Short explanation

When this directional second derivative stays positive, every direction bends upward; when it changes sign, some directions bend up and others down, which is the hallmark of a saddle.

Principal 1D slices

Restrict \(f\) to the two principal curvature directions. These slices reveal the local one-dimensional shape encoded by the Hessian eigenvalues.

eigenvalue slices

Short explanation

Along each eigenvector, the quadratic part becomes a simple parabola with coefficient equal to the corresponding eigenvalue. So the two slices show the essential local second-order structure.

signature map

Short explanation

The determinant decides whether the quadratic form keeps the same sign or changes sign. If it keeps the same sign, \(f_{xx}\) tells whether the local shape opens upward or downward.

Second-order model. Near any point, the Hessian determines the local quadratic correction to the tangent plane.
At critical points. When the gradient vanishes, the Hessian becomes the leading local term and therefore decides the local geometry—except in degenerate cases.
Degeneracy. If the Hessian is singular or zero, the usual second-order test may fail. Then higher-order terms decide the true local behavior.
