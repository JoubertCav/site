Inverse function theorem

The right picture for the theorem is a microscope: zoom near \(a\), replace \(F\) by its best linear map \(DF(a)\), and ask whether that linear map collapses any direction. If it does not, \(F\) is locally reversible.

local inverse
\[ F:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n,\quad F\in C^1, \qquad \det DF(a)\neq0 \] \[ \Longrightarrow \exists U\ni a,\ V\ni F(a): F|_U:U\to V\text{ is a }C^1\text{ diffeomorphism} \] \[ D(F^{-1})(F(a))=(DF(a))^{-1} \] \[ \det DF(a)=0 \quad\text{means the theorem gives no local inverse.} \]

Nonsingular Jacobian

Invertibility of \(DF(a)\) means the local linear map sends small disks to non-collapsed ellipses. No direction is lost.

Local diffeomorphism

The conclusion is local: around \(a\), \(F\) has a smooth inverse even if the same map may fail to be globally one-to-one elsewhere.

Singular failure

If \(\det DF(a)=0\), the theorem no longer applies. The examples show typical failures: folding or angle-doubling at the origin.

Jacobian microscope

This removes the nonlinear map and shows only \(h\mapsto DF(a)h\). A circle becomes an ellipse if \(DF(a)\) is nonsingular and collapses when singular.

linear core

Short explanation

The theorem works because the nonlinear map is close to its Jacobian at small scales. When the Jacobian is invertible, the nonlinear image inherits a local inverse.

Regular versus singular behavior

Regular points create one clean nearby preimage. Singular points can produce two nearby preimages or a flattened image direction.

failure modes

Short explanation

Singularity does not logically prove non-invertibility in every possible example, but it removes the theorem's guarantee. These maps show the canonical geometric failures.

IFT condition. \(F\in C^1\), same input/output dimension, and \(\det DF(a)\neq0\).
Geometric meaning. A small neighborhood around \(a\) is deformed without folding or flattening into a neighborhood around \(F(a)\).
Derivative of inverse. The inverse map has derivative \((DF(a))^{-1}\) at \(F(a)\).
