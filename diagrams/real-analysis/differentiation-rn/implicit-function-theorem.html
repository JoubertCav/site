Implicit function theorem

The theorem says that a level equation \(F(x,y)=c\) can be solved locally as a graph when the differential has full rank. In \(\mathbb R^2\), this means \(\nabla F(a)\neq 0\): the level set is a smooth curve, its tangent space is the kernel of \(DF(a)\), and one coordinate can be solved as a function of the other.

Singular warning

If the gradient vanishes, the theorem does not guarantee a graph.

Regular level set

When \(\nabla F(a)\neq 0\), the level set is a smooth one-dimensional manifold near \(a\): visually, just one clean curve with a well-defined tangent.

Local solvability

If \(F_y(a)\neq 0\), then the curve can be written as \(y=\varphi(x)\). If instead \(F_x(a)\neq 0\), then \(x=\psi(y)\). The right panel makes this local graph explicit.

Failure at singular points

When \(\nabla F(a)=0\), the regular level-set picture can fail: the set may pinch, cross, or stop being a graph. The theorem correctly refuses to conclude.

Tangent-space interpretation

For \(F:\mathbb R^2\to\mathbb R\), the derivative \(DF(a)\) is a row vector. Its kernel is the tangent line. The gradient is orthogonal to that kernel.

\(\ker DF(a)\)

Short explanation

The tangent line is the set of first-order displacements \(h\) that do not change the level value: \(DF(a)\,h=0\). This is why it is the kernel of the derivative.

Surface-section picture

The curve \(F(x,y)=c\) is the intersection of the surface \(z=F(x,y)\) with the horizontal plane \(z=c\). Regularity means the intersection is transverse and locally smooth.

\(z=F(x,y)\)

Short explanation

The 3D panel explains why the theorem is about solving equations locally: a regular cut of the surface by a horizontal plane produces a smooth intersection curve.

Test. In \(\mathbb R^2\), regularity is simply \(\nabla F(a)\neq 0\).
