Gradient, tangent planes, and level sets

The gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent, the tangent plane is the best local affine approximation to the graph, and the level set geometry is controlled by orthogonality: \(\nabla f(a)\) is normal to the contour through \(a\).

Gradient

The gradient lives in the \((x,y)\)-plane. It points in the direction where the function increases fastest, and its length is the maximal slope at the point.

Tangent plane

The tangent plane is the best local affine approximation to the surface. Very near \(a\), the graph and the plane are almost indistinguishable to first order.

Normals and level sets

The same gradient that controls steepest ascent is also normal to the contour through \(a\). This links 3D graph geometry to 2D level-set geometry.

Level sets and normal directions

The contour through \(a\) is the level set \(f(x,y)=f(a)\). The gradient is normal to that contour, and the directional derivative is its projection onto \(u\).

contour geometry

Short explanation

A level set is the set of points where the function has the same value. Moving along that contour does not change \(f\), so the tangent direction must be orthogonal to the gradient.

Directional derivative by angle

As the direction rotates, the slope follows \(D_u f(a)=|\nabla f(a)|\cos(\theta-\theta_\nabla)\).

cosine law

Short explanation

The directional derivative depends only on the angle between \(u\) and \(\nabla f(a)\). It is largest when \(u\) points with the gradient, zero when \(u\) is tangent to the level set, and most negative in the opposite direction.

3D tangent basis and normal

The tangent plane is spanned by \(r_x=(1,0,f_x)\) and \(r_y=(0,1,f_y)\). Their cross product gives a normal direction.

tangent space

Short explanation

The tangent plane is generated by two independent tangent vectors, one coming from changing \(x\) and one from changing \(y\). Their cross product produces a normal vector, which encodes the plane's orientation in space.

Orthogonality on the level set

Any tangent vector \(v\) to the contour satisfies \(\nabla f(a)\cdot v=0\). The gradient kills tangent motion along the level set.

\(\nabla f \perp T_aL_c\)

Short explanation

The identity \(\nabla f(a)\cdot v=0\) says that the gradient ignores motion along the contour. Only the component of a direction that points across level sets contributes to the change in the function value.

Gradient. It lives in the domain plane. Its direction is steepest ascent and its norm is the maximal directional derivative.
Tangent plane. The graph of \(f\) is locally approximated by the affine plane with slopes \(f_x(a)\) and \(f_y(a)\).
Level-set geometry. Contours are tangent to vectors orthogonal to the gradient, so the gradient is the canonical normal direction.
