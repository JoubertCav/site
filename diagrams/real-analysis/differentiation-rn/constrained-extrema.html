Constrained extrema

The constrained extremum problem asks where an objective \(f\) is largest or smallest while motion is restricted to a constraint manifold. The geometry combines 3D surfaces, 2D contours, normal directions, and second-order tests.

Normal cone view

For the filled feasible region, the normal cone is the ray \(\{\mu n(a):\mu\ge0\}\).

Lagrange multipliers

At a constrained extremum you cannot move in an allowed tangent direction and improve the value. So the objective gradient cannot have a tangential component: it must point in a normal direction to the constraint.

Constraint manifold

The constraint is a smooth curve \(M\subset\mathbb R^2\). Restricting \(f\) to \(M\) turns the problem into a one-variable problem \(\phi(\theta)=f(r(\theta))\).

Second-order test

Once \(\phi'(\theta_0)=0\), the sign of \(\phi''(\theta_0)\) distinguishes local minima from local maxima along the constraint.

2D contours and constraint tangency

A constrained extremum occurs where a contour of \(f\) is tangent to the constraint. The objective gradient is then parallel to the constraint normal and orthogonal to the tangent direction.

2D geometry

Short explanation

In the plane, the picture is easiest: the red curve is the constraint, the green curves are objective level sets, and a constrained critical point occurs exactly when the two are tangent.

Reduced 1D problem \(\phi(\theta)=f(r(\theta))\)

The constrained problem becomes a one-dimensional problem along the parameter \(\theta\). Its derivative and curvature give the first- and second-order tests.

1D restriction

Short explanation

Once the constraint is parameterized, the problem is classical calculus again: find where \(\phi'(\theta)=0\), then inspect \(\phi''(\theta)\) to determine whether the point is a constrained minimum or maximum.

Normal cone picture

Replace the equality manifold by the filled feasible region \(K\) bounded by the same curve. At a smooth boundary point, the normal cone is a ray generated by the outward normal.

first-order inequality view

Short explanation

For a smooth boundary, the normal cone is not a wide cone but a single ray. Boundary optima for inequality problems must align with that ray, up to sign depending on whether one minimizes or maximizes.

Tangent vs normal decomposition

Every objective gradient decomposes into a tangential part and a normal part relative to the constraint. At a constrained critical point the tangential part disappears.

orthogonal split

Short explanation

The tangent component tells you how much \(f\) changes if you move along the constraint. Constrained criticality means that this component is zero, leaving only the normal component.

Geometric condition. On a smooth manifold, constrained extrema happen where the objective gradient has no allowed tangential component.
Multiplier meaning. The number \(\lambda\) measures how strongly the objective gradient aligns with the active normal direction.
Second order. After the first-order tangency condition, the curvature of the restricted function decides min versus max along the constraint.
