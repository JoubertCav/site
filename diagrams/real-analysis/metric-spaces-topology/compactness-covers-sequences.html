Compactness:
covers and sequences

Two equivalent ways of seeing the same object in metric spaces: every open cover can be reduced to finitely many sets, and every sequence contains a convergent subsequence whose limit remains inside.

Central equivalence in ℝⁿ

K ⊂ ℝⁿ is compact
⇔ every open cover of K has a finite subcover
⇔ every sequence in K has a subsequence converging to a point of K
⇔ K is closed and bounded.

Sequential lens

A compact set traps infinite sequences tightly enough to force a convergent subsequence inside the set.

Definition

K compact
⇔ for every family of open sets {Uα}α∈A,
K ⊂ ⋃α∈AUα
implies K ⊂ Uα₁∪···∪Uαₘ.

This is the topological definition. It uses open sets, not sequences.

What to notice

Closed + bounded is the special simplification valid in ℝⁿ. The actual topological object is the finite-subcover property.

Exam summary

  • In ℝⁿ: compact ⇔ closed and bounded.
  • Metric spaces: compact ⇔ sequentially compact.
  • Not closed: sequences may converge to a missing boundary point.
  • Not bounded: sequences may escape with no convergent subsequence.
  • Finite sets: compact in every metric space.
