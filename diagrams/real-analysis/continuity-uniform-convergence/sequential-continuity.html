Sequential continuity

Continuity seen through convergent sequences: points approach the input, and their images must approach the value of the function.
Sequential criterion
\[ f \text{ continuous at } a \Longleftrightarrow \forall (x_n),\ x_n\to a \Rightarrow f(x_n)\to f(a). \]
Failure mode
\[ f \text{ not continuous at } a \Longleftrightarrow \exists (x_n):\ x_n\to a,\quad f(x_n)\not\to f(a). \]

What to notice

A sequence can approach \(a\) from the right, left, or both sides. A continuous function cannot distinguish these approaches in the limit.

For discontinuity, one carefully chosen sequence is enough to disprove continuity.

A removable discontinuity is subtle: \(f(x_n)\) may converge, but to the wrong value.

Exam-style summary

\[ x_n\to a \quad\Longrightarrow\quad f(x_n)\to f(a) \]
  • To prove continuity: take an arbitrary sequence \((x_n)\) with \(x_n\to a\).
  • To disprove continuity: exhibit one sequence \(x_n\to a\) with bad image behavior.
  • Jump: two one-sided limits disagree.
  • Removable: the punctured limit exists but differs from \(f(a)\).
  • Oscillatory: image sequences fail to settle.
Sequential continuity and topological continuity are equivalent in metric spaces.
