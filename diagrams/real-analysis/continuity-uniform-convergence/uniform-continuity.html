Uniform continuity

Ordinary continuity gives a \(\delta\) that may depend on the point. Uniform continuity gives one global \(\delta\) that works everywhere on the domain.

\[ \forall \varepsilon>0\ \exists \delta>0:\ |x-y|<\delta \Rightarrow |f(x)-f(y)|<\varepsilon \] \[ \text{same }\delta\text{ for all }x,y \]
Heine–Cantor. If \(K\subset\mathbb R^n\) is compact and \(f:K\to\mathbb R\) is continuous, then \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \(K\).

Failure pictures

Two typical ways uniform continuity can fail.

contrast

Noncompact growth

For \(x^2\) on \(\mathbb R\), distant regions need smaller and smaller local \(\delta_x\).

Boundary singularity

For \(1/x\) on \((0,1)\), local \(\delta_x\) collapses near \(0\).

Ordinary continuity. For each point \(a\) and each \(\varepsilon>0\), there exists a \(\delta_a\) that may depend on \(a\).
Uniform continuity. For each \(\varepsilon>0\), one \(\delta\) works simultaneously for every pair \(x,y\) in the domain.
Geometric test. The graph has no region where a fixed horizontal move can create a vertical jump larger than \(\varepsilon\).
