Sequence convergence

The limit is not where the first terms are. It is the point around which every sufficiently late tail is trapped.

Control object

ε–N
Definition.
xn → L ⇔ ∀ε > 0, ∃N ∈ ℕ : n ≥ N ⇒ |xn − L| < ε.
Choose ε. The task is to move N far enough so the whole tail lies inside the horizontal ε-band around L.

Uniqueness of limits

If two proposed limits are distinct, choose ε smaller than half their distance. Their neighborhoods cannot share the same eventual tail.
ε < |A − B|/2

Move the fake limit

If xn → A and xn → B with A ≠ B, choose η = |A − B|/3.
Eventually |xn−A| < η and |xn−B| < η.
Then |A−B| ≤ |A−xn| + |xn−B| < 2η < |A−B|, contradiction.

What to notice

visual logic
Convergence is eventual. Finitely many early terms do not matter.
ε is chosen first. N may depend on ε.
The tail must fit entirely. It is not enough to have infinitely many terms near L.
Uniqueness is geometric. Two distinct limits have disjoint small neighborhoods.
Boundedness follows from tail + finite beginning.

Common mistakes

Do not write ∃ε. The definition requires ∀ε > 0.
Do not let N depend on n. It depends on ε.
Do not prove only a subsequence converges. The whole tail must be trapped.
Do not confuse bounded with convergent. Every convergent sequence is bounded, but not conversely.
Exam core.

xn → L ⇔ ∀ε>0, ∃N∈ℕ : n≥N ⇒ |xn−L|<ε.

If xn→L and xn→M, then L=M.

If xn→L, then (xn) is bounded.

If xn→a and yn→b, then:
xn±yn→a±b,
xnyn→ab,
xn/yn→a/b if b≠0 and yn≠0 eventually.
To prove convergence in an exam, start from |xn−L|, dominate it by a simple expression, and choose N so the expression is smaller than ε.
