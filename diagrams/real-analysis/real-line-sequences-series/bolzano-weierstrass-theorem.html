Compactness intuition

Bolzano–Weierstrass for \(\mathbb R\) says bounded gives a real subsequential limit. Compactness adds: the limit stays inside the set.

\[\text{bounded} \Rightarrow \text{some subsequence has a real limit.}\]

Boundedness prevents escape to \(\pm\infty\). It does not say the whole sequence converges.

\[x_n\in K,\ x_{n_k}\to x,\ K\text{ closed}\Rightarrow x\in K.\]

Example: \(x_n=1/n\) lies in \((0,1)\), but its limit is \(0\notin(0,1)\).

\[K\subset\mathbb R\text{ compact}\Longleftrightarrow K\text{ closed and bounded}.\]

Sequentially: every sequence in \(K\) has a subsequence converging to a point of \(K\).

Maximal lesson: boundedness gives an accumulation point in the ambient line; compactness keeps the accumulation point inside the set.

What to notice

Convergence of the whole sequence is not required. The theorem only promises a well-chosen subsequence.

Accumulation point

A point \(x\) is detected by arbitrarily small neighborhoods containing infinitely many terms.

Exam summary

Bounded \(\Rightarrow\) nested intervals \(\Rightarrow\) choose increasing indices \(n_k\) \(\Rightarrow\) subsequence Cauchy \(\Rightarrow\) convergent in \(\mathbb R\).

The diagrams are finite simulations of an infinite theorem. The logical proof uses completeness of \(\mathbb R\).
