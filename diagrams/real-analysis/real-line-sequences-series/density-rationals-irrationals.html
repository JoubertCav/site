Density of rationals and irrationals

Every open interval, however small, contains both rational and irrational points. The decimal picture gives intuition; the order-theoretic proof uses the Archimedean property and does not depend on base ten.

Order-theoretic proof

The proof is geometric after multiplying the interval by a large integer.
rational construction
Choose n ∈ ℕ with n(b − a) > 1. Let m = ⌊na⌋ + 1. Then na < m < nb, so q = m/n ∈ (a,b).

Irrational by shifting

Use a tiny irrational displacement τ and then shift a rational point.
irrational construction
Choose τ = √2/k with 0 < τ < b − a. Pick r ∈ ℚ ∩ (a − τ, b − τ). Then ξ = r + τ is irrational and ξ ∈ (a,b).

Decimal intuition

Finite decimals are rational. At enough depth, a decimal grid point lands inside any interval.
Decimal expansion is intuition, not the theorem. Density is an order statement: between any two real numbers there are points of both types.

What to notice

The theorem says both sets are everywhere, even though they are disjoint.
1

ℚ has no open gap: every open interval touches it.

2

ℝ∖ℚ also has no open gap: irrational points are just as dense.

3

Density does not mean “many” in the cardinal sense. ℚ is countable; ℝ∖ℚ is uncountable.

4

Topologically: cl(ℚ)=ℝ and cl(ℝ∖ℚ)=ℝ.

Compact exam-style summary

A clean proof pattern to reproduce.
Rational density. Let a<b. By the Archimedean property, choose n∈ℕ such that n(b−a)>1. Put m=⌊na⌋+1. Then na<m≤na+1<nb, hence a<m/n<b.
Irrational density. Pick k∈ℕ so large that τ=√2/k<b−a. Since rationals are dense, choose r∈ℚ with a−τ<r<b−τ. Then ξ=r+τ∈(a,b), and ξ is irrational.
Topological form. A set D⊂ℝ is dense iff every nonempty open interval intersects D. Therefore ℚ and ℝ∖ℚ are both dense in ℝ.
