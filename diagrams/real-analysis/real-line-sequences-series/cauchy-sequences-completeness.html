Cauchy sequences and completeness

A sequence is Cauchy when its tail becomes internally small. Completeness says that in \(\mathbb R\), every internally small tail has a real point to converge to. In \(\mathbb Q\), a hole can remain.

Proof microscope · convergent ⇒ Cauchy

1
Assume convergence.

For a given ε, eventually |xₙ − L| < ε/2.

2
Take two late terms.

If m,n ≥ N, then both xₘ and xₙ lie in the same ε/2-neighborhood of L.

3
Use the triangle inequality.

|xₘ − xₙ| ≤ |xₘ − L| + |xₙ − L| < ε.

Convergence is external: terms approach a named point L. Cauchy is internal: late terms approach each other.

Completeness · why \(\mathbb R\) matters

\(\mathbb R\) complete ⇔ every Cauchy sequence of real numbers converges to a real number.
\(\mathbb Q\) not complete: qₙ ∈ \(\mathbb Q\), qₙ → √2, but √2 ∉ \(\mathbb Q\).

A Cauchy sequence can describe a missing point. Completeness is the axiom that the missing point is actually present.
