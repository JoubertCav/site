Seigniorage and the Inflation Tax

Seigniorage is the real revenue the government obtains from money creation. At low inflation, higher inflation can raise this revenue. But as inflation erodes real money balances, the tax base shrinks and a Laffer-curve logic emerges. If fiscal needs exceed the maximum attainable seigniorage, hyperinflation risk appears.

What to notice

  • Seigniorage is an inflation tax on holders of real money balances.
  • The tax base is \(m(\pi)\), which falls as inflation rises.
  • The revenue-maximizing inflation rate is not socially desirable; welfare losses grow before revenue peaks.
  • If fiscal need exceeds the top of the Laffer curve, the system may move toward unstable inflation or hyperinflation.

Economic interpretation

mechanism

Inflation tax

Higher inflation reduces the purchasing power of money, transferring resources to the issuer of money.

Laffer logic

Too much inflation shrinks money demand so much that seigniorage revenue falls rather than rises.

Hyperinflation risk

When expectations worsen and fiscal financing relies too heavily on money creation, the demand for money can collapse abruptly.

Exam-style summary

  • Seigniorage: \(s(\pi)=\pi\cdot m(\pi)\).
  • Inflation tax base: real money balances \(m(\pi)\).
  • With \(m(\pi)=kY e^{-\eta\pi}\), the peak occurs at \(\pi^*=1/\eta\).
  • If fiscal need lies above the maximum attainable seigniorage, inflation financing becomes unstable.
